PREFACE
The primary focus of any engineering work in the technical education system is to develop
the needed industry relevant competency and skills. With this in view, MSBTE embarked
on innovative “K” scheme curricula for engineering diploma programs with the outcome
based education through continuous inputs from socio economic sectors. The industry
experts during the consultation while preparing the Perspective Plan for diploma level
technical education categorically mentioned that the curriculum, which is revised and
implemented normally further revised after 4-5 years. Perceiving the technological
advancements being envisaged and faced by the industry in the present era are rapid and
curriculum needs to be revised by taking care of such advancements and therefore should
have a provision of accommodating continual changes. These views of industry experts
were well taken and further discussed in the academic committee of MSBTE, wherein it
was decided to have a dynamism in curriculum for imparting the latest technological
advancements in the respective field of engineering. In order to provide an opportunity to
students to learn the technological advancements, a course with a nomenclature of
“Emerging Trends in Electronics Engineering” is introduced in the 6th semester of
Electronics Engineering Group.
The technological advancements to be depicted in the course called emerging trends was a
challenging task and therefore it was decided to prepare a learning material with the
involvement of industrial and academic experts for its uniformity in the aspect of delivery,
implementation and evaluation. In the electronics sector, new applications are coming up
and it is mandatory for all technologists to be well versed in these developments to survive
and provide satisfactory and quality services to the society and industry. This course aims
to prepare the diploma graduates to be conversant with such emerging trends. The main
areas in which such developments encompass are Advanced Processors and Technology,
Smart Manufacturing Processes and Tools, Next Generation Telecom Network, IIoT and
Immersive Technologies and Drone systems and applications. Each unit in the course given
an insight to the learner about the latest development in the relevant fields.
This learning manual is designed to help all stakeholders, especially the students and
teachers and to develop in the student the pre-determined outcomes. It is expected to
explore further by both students and teachers, on the various topics mentioned in learning
manual to keep updated themselves about the advancements in related technology.

1.1.1 Features of a GPU
Significant features of GPU are as follows
1. Parallel Processing Power: GPU can handle thousands of simultaneous tasks, for
efficient graphics rendering.
2. High Floating-Point Performance – GPU has separate core for mathematical operations
like floating-point arithmetic.
3. Graphics Rendering: GPUs are designed to generate high-quality, smooth visuals for
games, movies, and interactive applications, ensuring a realistic experience.
4. High Memory Bandwidth – Equipped with GDDR6, HBM, or other high-speed memory
types for rapid data access.
5. Programmable Shaders – Vertex, geometry, pixel, and compute shaders enable flexible
rendering pipelines.
6. CUDA / OpenCL / Direct Compute Support – APIs that allow developers to run general-
purpose computations on GPUs (GPGPU computing). CUDA (Compute Unified Device
Architecture) is a parallel computing platform and application programming interface
(API) model.
7. Ray Tracing and AI Features – Modern GPUs include dedicated RT (Ray Tracing) cores
for realistic lighting and Tensor cores for AI-based tasks.
8. Multi-GPU and Scalability – Some systems support SLI/Crossfire or NV Link for
combining multiple GPUs. SLI and cross fire are technologies those allows two or more
graphic cards to work together to enhance gaming performance
9. Display Connectivity – HDMI (High Definition Multimedia Interface), Display Port,
and multi-monitor support with high refresh rates and resolutions (4K, 8K).
10. Energy Efficiency – Optimized architectures balance performance with thermal design
power (TDP).
11. Virtualization and Cloud Support – Many GPUs support GPU virtualization for cloud
gaming, AI, and virtual desktop infrastructure.

1.1.3 Superscalar concept
Superscalar architecture is an advanced microprocessor design which allows CPU to
execute multiple instructions per clock cycle. This is done by providing multiple

Concepts of Superscalar Architecture
a. Instruction-Level Parallelism (ILP): Superscalar processors can run several
instructions at the same time, as long as the instructions are independent of each other.
b. Multiple Execution Units: The processor has different units for different tasks (like
math, floating-point, or memory). It can send instructions to the right unit in the same
clock cycle.
c. Instruction Scheduler: The CPU checks for dependencies, avoids conflicts, and decides
which instructions can run in parallel, keeping all units working efficiently
Working of Superscalar architecture
A superscalar processor processes instructions as follows:
Fig 1.4 Superscalar architecture (Processor with two execution units)
a. Instruction Fetch: At a time, several instructions are fetched from memory at once.
b. Instruction Decode: Instructions are decoded in parallel, and the scheduler determines
which can be executed simultaneously.
c. Dispatch and Execution: Independent instructions are sent to available execution units.
Different units for are available for integer and floating-point operations.
d. Out-of-Order and Parallel Execution: Instructions are executed as soon as dependencies
are resolved, not strictly in program order but the results are arranged in such a way,
which ensures that outputs appear as if executed sequentially.
e. Writeback: Results of instructions are written back, using a reorder buffer to maintain
program order.

1.2 Microcontroller ESP 32 :
The ESP32 is a very versatile, low-cost, robust, and low-power-based System On a Chip
general-purpose microcontroller with a dual-core processor, set of peripherals, large
memory capacity, and integrated Wi-Fi and Bluetooth connectivity capabilities. This
device provides a wide range of features like a large memory capacity, a dual-core
processor and a set of peripherals. As compared to other of microcontrollers, these devices
offer features like a superior power balance, and flexibility. ESP32 can be programmed in
by using different ways as it supports various programming environments like Arduino
IDE, LUA, MicroPython, JavaScript, Espressif IDF, and Platform IO IDE.

1.2.4 ESP 32 simple programs and applications
Tools for Programming ESP32
1) ESP-IDF (Espressif IoT Development Framework):
This is Espressif's official framework, providing a comprehensive SDK for
developing applications in C and C++. It offers low-level access to the hardware and
is suitable for performance-critical applications.
2) Arduino IDE:
The popular Arduino IDE can be used to program the ESP32 through the Espressif
Arduino core. This allows developers to leverage the familiar Arduino programming
language (C/C++) and a vast ecosystem of libraries.
3) MicroPython:
This is a lean implementation of Python 3 optimized for microcontrollers. It provides
a more high-level approach to programming the ESP32, facilitating rapid prototyping
and development.
4) PlatformIO:

Sketch:
A sketch is a program written with the Arduino IDE. Sketches are saved on the
development computer as text files with the file extension .ino. Arduino Software (IDE)
pre-1.0 saved sketches with the extension .pde.
The programming cycle on Arduino is basically as follows:
1. Plug the board into a USB port on computer.
2. Write a sketch that will bring the board to life.
3. Upload this sketch to the board through the USB connection and wait a couple
of seconds for the board to restart.
4. The board executes the sketch that is written.
C/C++ program consists of two functions:
• s etup() : This function is called once when a sketch starts after power-up or reset. It is
used to initialize variables, input and output pin modes, and other libraries needed in
the sketch. It is analogous to the function main() .
• loop() : After setup() function exits (ends), the loop() function is executed
repeatedly in the main program. It controls the board until the board is powered off or
is reset. It is analogous to the function while(1) in C Program.

1.4 Quantum Computing:
Quantum computing is a emerging field that uses the principles of quantum mechanics to
process information. As contrast to classical computers, that uses bits with either value of
0 or 1, quantum computers use quantum bits or qubits, which can exist in a state of 0, 1, or
both simultaneously which exponentially increases the computational power for specific
types of problem for which the classical computers are inefficient. Quantum computers are
particularly suitable for solving complex, high-dimensional, and probabilistic problems.
1.4.1 Qubit (Quantum Bit)
The qubit is the fundamental unit of information in quantum computing. It's the quantum
analog of the classical bit.
Mathematically, a qubit’s state is represented as:
|𝜑 >=∝ |0 > +𝛽|1 >
where α and 𝛽 are complex probability amplitudes, and | ∝ |2 + |𝛽|2 = 1
Quantum Phenomena:
1. Superposition: As contrast to classical bit, which can only be in one of two states (0 or
1), a qubit can exist in a superposition of both states simultaneously. A qubit can be in
a combination of both 0 and 1 states at the same time.
2. Entanglement: Qubits can be linked together in such a way that the state of one qubit
instantly influences the state of another, no matter the distance between them.
Ex: In a pair of entangled qubits, if one qubit is found to be |0⟩, then the other is |1⟩, and
vice versa. This creates powerful correlations that are used in quantum algorithms.

3. Interference: Quantum states can combine like waves, amplifying correct paths to a
solution and cancelling out incorrect ones.
4. Exponential Power: The true power comes from scaling. With *n* qubits, thanks to

For example:
a) 2 qubits can be in a superposition of 4 states: |00⟩, |01⟩, |10⟩, |11⟩.

2.1 Surface Mount Technology (SMT):
A Surface-Mount Technology (SMT) is a technology or method for producing electronic
circuits. In this technology or method the electronic device/components are directly placed
or mounted onto the surface of the PCB, hence is called Surface-Mount Technology (SMT).
SMT was developed to minimize the manufacturing cost and to make efficient use of PCB
space. It has largely replaced the Through-Hole Technology (THT) especially in devices
that need to be small or flat. With SMT both sides of a PCB can be used when required.
SMT components are smaller in size than THT/ Pinup components since they have either
smaller leads or no leads. (Fig. 2.1) This makes it easier to shrink the PCB size.
For example, SMD resistors are available in a variety of sizes, including 0805 (0.8mm long
by 0.5mm broad), 0603 and 0402.

than through-hole components, allowing for a much higher number of parts to be
placed on a single PCB.
● Surface Mounting: Components are connected directly to the surface of the PCB
rather than being threaded through drilled holes.
● Automation and Efficiency: SMT is ideal for automated manufacturing processes,
leading to increased speed, higher efficiency, and reduced labor cost.
● Lightweight and Compact Designs: The smaller component size and ability to
mount on both sides of the PCB result in significantly lighter and smaller final
products.
● Improved Electrical Performance: Reduced lead lengths in SMT components
lead to lower parasitic inductance and capacitance, improving electrical
performance and enabling high-frequency applications.
● Higher Reliability: SMT assembly processes, when properly implemented, can
result in high-quality solder joints with low reject ratios, contributing to higher
overall product reliability.
● Versatile Component Packaging: SMT components come in various forms and
packages, including tiny resistors, capacitors, and integrated circuits, as well as Ball
Grid Arrays (BGAs).

Advantages of SMT

2.3 Modern Electronic Assembly and Manufacturing Process:
In modern electronic assembly the Surface Mount Devices (SMD) are placed
automatically onto the surface of the board.
Before the PCB manufacturing process, the PCB design and its functionality is
checked as per the specific requirements. The PCB assembly company checks the
PCB design file for any issues that may affect the PCB's functionality or
manufacturability. This is called a design for manufacturability check, or DFM
check.
The DFM check looks at all the design specifications of a PCB. Specifically, this
DFM checks for any missing, redundant or potentially problematic features. Any
of these issues may severely and negatively influence the functionality of the final
project. For example, one common PCB design flaw is leaving too little spacing
between PCB components. This can result in shorts and other malfunctions.
By identifying potential problems before manufacturing begins, DFM checks
minimize the manufacturing cost as these checks cut down on the number of
scrapped boards. As part of quality at a low cost, DFM checks have become
standard with every PCB assembly for an automatic electronic assembly system
contributing to high speed and accuracy.

common side effect of this movement is shorts, as misplaced components can sometimes
connect portions of the circuit that should not connect.
The Automatic Optical Inspection (AOI) machine runs a number of quality checks for the
boards visually, such as component alignment and checking for solder bridges (Fig 2.19).

Automatic Optical Inspection:
Automatic optical inspection is used for inspection of large batches of PCB Assembly. An
automatic optical inspection machine, also known as an AOI machine, uses a series of high-
powered cameras to "see" PCBs. These cameras are arranged at different angles to view
solder connections. Different quality solder connections reflect light in different ways,
allowing the AOI to recognize a lower-quality solder. The AOI does this at a very high
speed, allowing it to process a high quantity of PCBs in a relatively short time.
Whether an inspection finds one of these mistakes or not, the next step of the process is to
test the part to make sure it does what it's supposed to do. This involves testing the PCB
connections for quality. Boards requiring programming or calibration require even more
steps to test proper functionality.
Such inspections can occur regularly after the reflow process to identify any potential
problems. These regular checks can ensure that errors are found and fixed as soon as
possible, which helps both the manufacturer and the designer save time, labor and
materials.

2.5 Introduction to Open-Source Assembly and Testing (OSAT):

Open-Source Assembly and Testing (OSAT) is a modern approach in the

community support to assemble and test electronic chips.

In Open-Source Assembly and Testing (OSAT), Assembly (i.e. packaging a small
silicon chip (die) into a form that can be connected and used in circuits) and Testing
(i.e. To check whether the assembled chips work correctly and meet performance
standards) processes are carried out using free and publicly available software tools,
data libraries, and open design standards. This approach allows students, researchers,
and engineers to collaborate, innovate, and reduce development costs while ensuring
transparency and accessibility in semiconductor design and manufacturing.

3.1 NGN Architecture:
The general idea behind the NGN is that one network transports all information and
services (Voice, data, and all sorts of media such as video) by encapsulating these into IP
packets, similar to those used on the Internet. NGNs are commonly built around the Internet
Protocol, and therefore the term all IP is also sometimes used to describe the transformation
of formerly telephone-centric networks toward NGN.
3.1.1 NGN Basic terms:
1) NGN: NGN is a packet-based network able to provide telecommunication services and
able to make use of multiple broadbands, QoS-enabled transport technologies in which
service-related functions are independent from underlying transport related
technologies.
2) Application network interface (ANI): Interface which provides a channel for
interactions and exchanges between applications and NGN elements. The ANI offers
capabilities and resources needed for the realization of applications.
3) Application programming interface (API): An API provides a set of interfaces from an
application environment to an execution environment. The execution environment
provides
services to the application environment.
4) NGN service stratum: NGN part which provides the user functions that transfer service-
related data and functions. It controls and manages service resources and network
services to enable user services and applications.
5) NGN transport stratum: NGN part which provides the user functions that transfer data
and the functions. It controls and manages transport resources to carry such data
between terminating entities.
6) Open service environment capabilities: Capabilities provided by an open service
environment to enable enhanced and flexible service creation and provisioning based
on the use of standards interfaces.
7) Service: A set of functions and facilities offered to a user by a provider.
8) Authorized account: A profile of the entity which a NGN end user can subscribe for
accessing the information via messaging exchange. Authorized means the account is
sanctioned and recognized by the service provider (third-party provider) and it is
always available to be accessed. The profile is a numeric string which includes the
identity of the entity and the service information of the entity.
9) Authorized account messaging service: A messaging service through which third-party
provider can register to an authorized account and interact with the account subscribers
via messaging exchange.

3.3 NGN Core
The next-generation core network is a single converged IP network, which can carry
multimedia signals including voice and data. Presently Multi-Protocol Label Switching
(MPLS) and Resilient Packet Ring (RPR) are some of the transport technologies adopted.
The evolution to a next-generation core network promises significant savings in terms of
bandwidth saving in long run and provides a stable platform for converged services.
3.3.1 NGN Core concepts
● Packet-based transfer: Unlike traditional networks that use circuit switching (reserving a
dedicated path for the duration of a call), NGNs use packet-based transfer. Information is
broken into small, individually routed data packets, which allows for more efficient use of
network resources.
● Separation of control and transport layers: This layered architecture is a fundamental
concept.
○ Transport layer: The lower layer handles connectivity and includes the access and core
networks, which are largely based on IP.
○ Control layer: The middle layer, also called the intelligence layer, handles session control,
signaling, and call control functions using soft switches and media gateway controllers
(MGCs).
○ Service layer: The upper layer provides the platform for service creation and deployment,
separate from the underlying transport infrastructure. This decoupling allows for the rapid
development of new services and applications.
● Access-agnostic nature: NGN is designed to be independent of the access technology used
by the end-user. It supports a wide range of access methods, including Digital Subscriber
line (DSL), cable, fiber-optic, and wireless technologies like 3G, 4G, and 5G.
● Media gateways: These are key network components that convert data between different
types of networks. A media gateway might terminate both time-division multiplexing
(TDM) and IP traffic, enabling interworking with legacy PSTN networks.
Maharashtra State Board of Technical Education, Mumbai
86


=== PAGE 94 ===
Emerging Trends in Electronics 316337
● IP Multimedia Subsystem (IMS): A standardized architecture for delivering multimedia
services over an IP network. IMS uses the Session Initiation Protocol (SIP) to manage and
control multimedia sessions for both fixed and mobile users.
3.3.2 NGN core features
● Service convergence: NGN combines multiple services—voice, data, video, and
multimedia—onto a single, unified IP network infrastructure. This eliminates the need for
separate, dedicated networks for each service.
● Quality of Service (QoS): NGN provides mechanisms to ensure high-quality service for
different types of traffic. It prioritizes data, such as real-time voice and video, to prevent
service degradation.
● Mobility: The architecture supports generalized mobility, allowing users to access
consistent services ubiquitously, regardless of their location or the access network they are
using.
● Open interfaces: NGNs use open, standards-based interfaces between layers. This
encourages a competitive market by enabling third-party developers and vendors to create
new applications and services, increasing innovation.
● Enhanced security: The infrastructure incorporates robust security mechanisms to protect
against various threats. These include advanced encryption, firewalls, and intrusion
detection and prevention systems.
● Scalability: By separating the control and service layers from the transport layer, NGN is
highly scalable. It can accommodate a growing number of users, devices, and traffic
demands by adding resources to specific layers as needed.

3.3.4 MPLS
MPLS (multi-protocol label switching) is used at core transport technology used in NGN
network. MPLS provides faster switching, propagation delay is less.
In NGN, a packet of a connectionless network layer protocol travels from one router to the
next, each router makes an independent forwarding decision for that packet. That is, each
router analyzes the packets header, and each router runs a network layer routing algorithm
and independently chooses a next hop for the packet, based on its packet's header.
Packet headers contain considerably more information than is needed simply to choose the
next hop. Choosing the next hop can therefore be thought of as the composition of two
functions.
a. The first function partitions the entire set of possible packets into a set of Forwarding
Equivalence Classes (FECs).
b. The second maps each FEC to a next hop.
Elements of MPLS: Fig 3.9 shows the MPLS architecture.
1) Labels: A label is a short, fixed length, locally significant identifier which is used to
identify a FEC. The label which is put on a particular packet represents the Forwarding
Equivalence Class to which that packet is assigned. Most commonly, a packet is assigned
to a FEC based on its network layer destination address. Each IP destination network has
a different label which has local significance. Label for a destination network changes in
each hop.
2) Label Switch Router:
A label switch router (LSR) is a router that supports MPLS. It is capable of understanding
MPLS labels and of receiving and transmitting a labeled packet on a data link. Three
kinds of LSRs exist in an MPLS network:
a. Provider Edge [PE] Router/Ingress LSRs: Ingress LSRs receive a packet that is not
labeled yet, insert a label (stack) in front of the packet, and send it on a data link.
b. Provider Edge [PE] Router/Egress LSRs: Egress LSRs receive labeled packets,
remove the label(s), and send them on a data link. Ingress and egress LSRs are edge
LSRs.
c. Core Edge [CE] Router/Intermediate LSRs: Intermediate LSRs receive an incoming

d. Upstream and Downstream LSRs
An LSR can do the three operations: PUSH/adding labels, POP/remove labels, or
SWAP/interchanging labels.
i. It must be able to pop one or more labels (remove one or more labels from the top of
the label stack) before switching the packet out.
ii. An LSR must also be able to push one or more labels onto the received packet.
iii. If the received packet is already labeled, the LSR pushes one or more labels onto the
label stack and switches out the packet.
iv. If the packet is not labeled yet, the LSR creates a label stack and pushes it onto the
packet. An LSR must also be able to swap a label. This simply means that when a
labeled
packet is received, the top label of the label stack is swapped with a new label and the
packet is switched on the outgoing data link.
v. An LSR that pushes labels onto a packet that was not labeled yet is called an imposing
LSR because it is the first LSR to impose labels onto the packet.
vi. One that is doing imposition is ingress LSR. An LSR that removes all labels from the
labeled packet before switching out the packet is a disposing LSR.
vii. One that does disposition is an egress LSR.
e. Label Switch path
A label switched path (LSP) is a sequence of LSRs that switch a labeled packet through
an MPLS network or part of an MPLS network. Basically, the LSP is the path through the
MPLS network or a part of it that packets take. The first LSR of an LSP is the ingress LSR
for that LSP, whereas the last LSR of the LSP is the egress LSR. All the LSRs in between
the ingress and egress LSRs are the intermediate LSRs. LSP is unidirectional. The flow
of labeled packets in the other direction right to left between the same edge LSRs would
be another LSP.

3.4.2 Optical Line Termination (OLT)
Definition: Optical Line Terminal or optical line termination is a device that basically acts

and manages the transmission and reception of information across the overall network.
The primary function of an OLT
● to convert electrical signals into optical signals and vice versa.
● Converting the standard signals used by a FiOS service provider to the frequency
and framing used by the PON system.
● Coordinating the multiplexing between the conversion devices on the optical
network terminals (ONTs) located on the customers' premises.
This conversion allows data to be transmitted over long distances using fiber-optic cables,
which are more efficient and reliable than traditional copper cables. The OLT also manages
the flow of data between the central office and the end users, assisting each user receiving
the appropriate amount of bandwidth.
The OLT contains a central processing unit (CPU), passive optical network cards, a
gateway router (GWR) and voice gateway (VGW) uplink cards. It can transmit a data
signal to users at 1490 nanometers (nm). That signal can serve up to 128 ONTs at a range
of up to 12.5 miles by using optical splitters.

3.5.1 Synchronous Digital Hierarchy (SDH):
Synchronous digital hierarchy (SDH) are standardized protocols that transfer multiple
digital bit streams synchronously over optical fiber using lasers or highly coherent from
light emitting diodes (LEDs). At low transmission rates data can also be transferred via an
electrical interface. SONET and SDH, which are essentially the same, were originally
designed to transport circuit mode communications (e.g., E1, E3) from a variety of different
sources, but they were primarily designed to support real-time, uncompressed, circuit-
switched voice encoded in PCM format. SDH allowed for the simultaneous transport of
many different circuits of differing origin within a single framing protocol. SDH is not a
communications protocol in itself, but a transport protocol.

4.1.2. Functions of Cyber-Physical System (CPS) Components
IoT works on the foundation of Cyber-Physical Systems (CPS), which combine physical
and digital (cyber) elements.
Internet of Things is about connecting "Things" (Objects and Machines) to the internet and
eventually to each other; while Cyber Physical Systems (CPS) is integration of
computation, networking and physical process. CPS is more related to physical objects and
machines, while the IoT is a little bit more abstract and allows the integration of service
and not only physical objects. CPS is more concentrated on concrete development scenarios
and does not target of providing data outside the original scenario. IoT make its data and
services available more open. All IoT devices are Cyber Physical Systems, but CPSs are
not necessarily connected to the Internet and thus, not necessarily IoT devices. Cyber
Physical System (CPS) is a mechatronic system in which entities are connected to each
other through wired or wireless solutions with means of information and communication
technology. Note that synonym for CPS is Industry 4.0 (i.e., a fourth generation of
Industrial Revolution). Cyber-Physical Systems collects a vast amount of knowledge of the
environment by using sensors. These sensors are connected to all the distributed knowledge
in the environment. This process can enable a more accurate action and task. In a proper
definition, Cyber-Physical Systems consists of computation, communication and control
components tightly combined with physical processes of different domains such as
mechanical, electrical, and chemical.

Working- A mixed reality system blends the real world with virtual objects to create a
seamless experience. First, a camera or sensor captures the real 3D environment and
projects it onto a 2D display. The camera’s internal settings (like focal length and lens
curvature) and external settings (like its position and viewing direction) determine how the
real scene is viewed.
Next, a computer graphics system (such as OpenGL) generates virtual objects. To place

like depth, perspective, and camera orientation. This data helps compute the virtual
camera’s position and projection, ensuring virtual objects align naturally with the real
environment.

producing a mixed reality output. The result is a display where virtual and real elements
coexist and interact realistically, creating an immersive and convincing experience.

5.1 Types of Drones
Drones (UAVs) Unmanned Aerial Vehicle can be classified into four major types based
on their aerodynamic design and flight mechanism:
A. Multi-Rotor Drones
B. Single-Rotor Drones
C. Fixed-Wing Drones
D. Hybrid (VTOL- Vertical Take-Off and Landing drone) Drones
A. Multi-Rotor Drones
A multirotor drone is an unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) that achieves lift, thrust, and
maneuverability through the use of multiple rotors and motors. Unlike fixed-wing aircraft,
which rely on forward motion to generate lift, multirotor drones are capable of vertical
takeoff and landing (VTOL) and can hover in a fixed position, making them particularly
suitable for applications that require stability, precise positioning, and maneuverability in
confined or complex environments.
Fig 5.1: Typical Multirotor Drones
(Courtesy: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/293652511)

Multirotor drones are classified based on the number of rotors they employ. The most
common configuration is the quadcopter, which uses four rotors arranged symmetrically to
provide balanced lift and stability. Other configurations include hexacopters, which use six
rotors and offer higher payload capacity and redundancy, and octocopters, which feature
eight rotors for maximum lift and reliability, allowing continued flight even in the event of
a motor failure.
The principle of operation of a multirotor drone is based on differential thrust. Each rotor
is powered by an individual brushless motor, and by varying the speed of each motor, the
drone can perform all basic movements, including ascending, descending, tilting forward
or backward (pitch), tilting sideways (roll), and rotating around its vertical axis (yaw). This
precise control mechanism enables the drone to hover steadily, execute complex
maneuvers, and maintain stability even under external disturbances such as wind gusts.
Fig 5.1 illustrates a typical multirotor drone, highlighting its main components including
the frame, motors, propellers, landing gears, and central flight controller. This diagram
helps in understanding the symmetrical layout of the rotors and the basic structure that
contributes to stability and control.
Multirotor drones are widely used in a variety of applications. In aerial photography and
videography, they provide a stable platform for capturing high-quality images and videos.
In surveillance and security operations, they allow for real-time monitoring of urban areas,
industrial zones, and borders. They are also employed for inspection and mapping of
infrastructure such as power lines, pipelines, agricultural fields, and construction sites.
Furthermore, multirotor drones are used in delivery systems for transporting lightweight
payloads, as well as in research, disaster management, and search-and-rescue operations
due to their maneuverability and ability to hover in critical areas.
Despite their versatility, multirotor drones have certain limitations. Their flight time is
generally limited because multiple motors consume significant amounts of energy. They
also have lower maximum speed and range compared to fixed-wing drones, and their
payload capacity is restricted by the number and size of the rotors.
B. Single-Rotor Drones
A single-rotor drone is an unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) that uses a single large main
rotor to generate lift, similar to a conventional helicopter, along with a smaller tail rotor to
maintain stability and control the yaw motion. Unlike multirotor drones, which rely on
multiple rotors for lift and maneuvering, single-rotor drones achieve efficient lift with
fewer rotors, allowing them to carry heavier payloads over longer distances and with longer
flight durations.

The main rotor provides both lift and propulsion, while the tail rotor counteracts the torque
produced by the spinning main rotor, preventing the drone from spinning uncontrollably.
Flight maneuvers such as pitch, roll, yaw, and vertical motion are controlled by adjusting
the collective and cyclic pitch of the main rotor blades and the thrust of the tail rotor. This
design makes single-rotor drones highly efficient in energy usage and capable of longer
flight times compared to multirotor drones of similar weight.
Figure 5.2 illustrates a typical single-rotor drone, highlighting the main rotor, tail rotor,
frame, landing skids, and central flight controller. This figure helps students visualize how
the single large rotor generates lift while the tail rotor maintains directional stability.
Single-rotor drones are widely used in applications that require heavy payload lifting,
extended flight duration, and energy efficiency. Common applications include aerial
photography and videography in large areas, agricultural spraying, cargo delivery,
surveying and mapping, and search-and-rescue operations in remote or challenging
environments. Additionally, these drones are often used in industrial inspections where
longer flight time and larger payload capacity are required to carry specialized sensors or
equipment.
Despite their advantages, single-rotor drones have certain limitations. They require a more
complex mechanical design, including the rotor pitch control mechanisms, which
increases maintenance requirements. They are also less stable at low speeds and require
more skill to operate safely compared to multirotor drones. Additionally, the tail rotor
adds mechanical complexity and can be a point of vulnerability during operation.

A fixed-wing drone is an unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) that generates lift through
stationary wings, similar to conventional airplanes. Unlike multirotor or single-rotor

them to achieve efficient, long-duration flight over large distances. They are not capable of

The wings of a fixed-wing drone are designed to produce lift when air flows over them,

D. Hybrid Drones (VTOL – Vertical Take-Off and Landing)
A hybrid drone is an unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) that combines the features of
multirotor drones and fixed-wing drones, allowing it to achieve vertical takeoff and landing
(VTOL) while also benefiting from the aerodynamic efficiency of fixed-wing flight. This
hybrid design provides the flexibility of hovering and vertical operations along with the
ability to cover longer distances at higher speeds.
Fig 5.4: Typical Hybrid VTOL Drone
(Courtesy: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/324865703/figure)
Hybrid drones are equipped with multiple rotors for vertical lift and a fixed-wing structure
for forward flight. During takeoff and landing, the rotors provide the necessary thrust to lift
the drone vertically from the ground. Once airborne, the drone transitions to fixed-wing
flight, where the wings generate lift, and a propeller provides forward thrust, reducing
energy consumption and increasing flight endurance. The flight controller coordinates this
transition between rotor-based and wing-based flight, ensuring stability and smooth
maneuvering throughout the operation.

Hybrid drones are ideal for missions requiring both vertical takeoff and long-range flight.
Common applications include aerial surveying, environmental monitoring, cargo delivery
in urban and remote areas, search-and-rescue operations, and military reconnaissance. The
combination of rotor-based VTOL and wing-based efficient cruising allows hybrid drones
to access confined spaces for takeoff and landing, then travel long distances quickly,
making them highly versatile for diverse operational scenarios.
Despite their versatility, hybrid drones involve more complex mechanical and control
systems compared to single-purpose UAVs. The transition from vertical to forward flight
requires precise coordination by the flight controller, and the dual propulsion system adds
to the weight, cost, and maintenance requirements.

5.2 Hardware Components of Drones
A drone is made up of several hardware components that work together to enable stable
flight, navigation, and data collection. These components can be grouped into structural
parts, propulsion system, control system, sensors, and power system.
A. Frame
A drone frame is the primary structural component of an unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV)
that provides the mechanical foundation and support for all other components, including
motors, propellers, flight controllers, electronic speed controllers (ESCs), cameras,
sensors, and batteries. The frame determines the overall shape, size, and configuration of

172


=== PAGE 180 ===
Emerging Trends in Electronics 316337
the drone, influencing its stability, maneuverability, payload capacity, and aerodynamic
efficiency.
Fig 5.5: Different types of drone frames
(Courtesy: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/305273853/)
Drone frames are designed to be lightweight yet strong, typically constructed from
materials such as carbon fiber, aluminum, or reinforced plastics. The choice of material
affects the drone’s durability, vibration resistance, and overall flight performance. Frames
come in various configurations, depending on the type of drone and its intended
application. For example, multirotor drones often have X-shaped or H-shaped frames to
support multiple rotors symmetrically, whereas single-rotor and hybrid drones have frames
designed to support a central main rotor and additional structural elements.
The frame serves several critical functions. It provides mounting points for motors and
propellers, ensures proper alignment and balance, and protects delicate electronic
components from physical damage during takeoff, flight, and landing. The geometry and
Maharashtra State Board of Technical Education, Mumbai

Emerging Trends in Electronics 316337
design of the frame also influence the drone’s aerodynamics and vibration characteristics,
which are essential for stable flight and accurate sensor readings.
Figure 5.5 shows a typical drone frame, highlighting its key structural components,
mounting points for motors and sensors, and integration with other essential systems. This
figure helps students understand how the frame forms the backbone of the drone, providing
support and rigidity while enabling modular assembly of all electronic and mechanical
components.
In addition to mechanical support, the frame contributes to the overall efficiency and safety
of the drone. A well-designed frame reduces drag, minimizes weight, and allows for
effective distribution of payloads. It also plays a role in impact absorption during rough
landings or minor collisions, protecting critical components and increasing the drone’s
operational lifespan.
B. Propellers
Propellers are one of the most essential components of a drone, responsible for generating
the aerodynamic thrust required for lift and movement. They function on the principle of
aerodynamic lift, similar to how an aircraft wing operates — by rotating at high speed, the
blades push air downwards, creating an upward force that enables the drone to rise and stay
airborne. The efficiency, stability, and maneuverability of a drone are highly dependent on

A typical drone propeller consists of two or more blades attached to a central hub, which
is mounted directly onto the motor shaft. When the motor spins, the propeller blades cut
through the air, generating thrust. By varying the rotational speed of each propeller, the
drone can ascend, descend, hover, or change direction. In multirotor drones, pairs of
propellers rotate in opposite directions — clockwise (CW) and counterclockwise (CCW)
— to balance torque and maintain stability during flight.
Propellers are commonly made from plastic, carbon fiber, or composite materials. Plastic
propellers are lightweight and inexpensive, making them suitable for small hobby drones,
while carbon fiber propellers are preferred for professional and industrial applications due
to their superior strength, rigidity, and vibration resistance. The diameter and pitch of the
propeller are key design parameters — larger diameters provide more thrust but require
stronger motors, whereas higher pitch angles increase speed but may reduce stability and
control precision.
The number of propellers used depends on the drone type. For example, quadcopters have
four propellers, hexacopters have six, and octocopters have eight. Each configuration
affects the drone’s load-carrying capacity, flight endurance, and stability. Balanced
propellers are crucial for smooth operation; any imbalance can lead to vibrations, reduced
efficiency, and potential damage to the motor or frame.

control.
In addition to lift generation, propellers play a vital role in maneuvering and stability. By
adjusting the speed of individual propellers through the flight controller, the drone can
perform complex movements such as yaw (rotation), pitch (forward/backward tilt), and roll
(sideways tilt). The precision in propeller control directly determines the flight
responsiveness and accuracy of the UAV.

predominantly used due to their high power-to-weight ratio, efficiency, low

F. Gimbal
A Gimbal is a mechanical stabilization device used in drones to keep the camera steady

and maintaining camera orientation.

process real-time data to counteract motion. They are commonly used in aerial
photography, videography, surveying, and mapping applications to produce high-quality,
vibration-free images and videos.
Gimbals are available in different materials such as aluminum alloy and carbon fiber,
ensuring a balance between strength and weight. They are designed to carry various

Communication Protocols for Drone Transmitters and Receivers
The radio transmitter and receiver form the primary communication link between the drone
pilot and the unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV). The transmitter, often referred to as the
remote controller, is a handheld device that sends control signals through specific
frequency bands to the receiver mounted on the drone. These signals correspond to flight
commands such as throttle, yaw, pitch, and roll, allowing the operator to maneuver the
drone in real time.
Modern transmitters operate on the 2.4 GHz or 5.8 GHz frequency bands, which provide
stable, interference-resistant communication suitable for both indoor and outdoor flight.
The receiver interprets these transmitted signals and forwards them to the flight controller,
which then adjusts the drone’s motors and servos accordingly. Communication between
the transmitter and receiver is established through digital transmission protocols, ensuring
low latency and reliable data transfer even at longer distances.
Several communication protocols are commonly used in drones. The most prevalent among
hobbyist and professional drones include PWM (Pulse Width Modulation), PPM (Pulse
Position Modulation), SBUS (Serial Bus), and DSM/DSM2 systems. PWM provides
individual signal lines for each channel and is easy to implement but is limited in terms of
response speed. PPM combines multiple channels into a single signal line, reducing wiring
complexity. SBUS, a digital serial protocol introduced by Futaba, allows up to 16 channels
to be transmitted using a single wire with faster response times and reduced latency, making
it highly suitable for advanced multirotor drones. FrSky and Spektrum systems use similar
serial communication standards to achieve efficient control transmission.

Drone operations in India are classified according to airspace sensitivity and surrounding
population density to ensure the safety and security of both aerial and ground environments.
The Directorate General of Civil Aviation (DGCA) divides Indian airspace into four
distinct zones — Red, Yellow (Inner and Outer), and Green — each defined by specific
operational permissions and restrictions.

The Red Zone represents highly sensitive or restricted airspace where the operation of
drones is strictly prohibited. These areas generally include airports, international borders,
military installations, nuclear power plants, and high-security government establishments.
Drone flights in these zones are not permitted under normal circumstances, as they pose
potential threats to national security and air safety. Any drone activity within these regions
can only occur with explicit authorization from the relevant central authorities, usually for
defense, emergency response, or research purposes. Violations in red zones are treated as
serious legal offenses under the Indian Aircraft Act and may attract severe penalties.
Surrounding the red zones is the Inner Yellow Zone, which serves as a protective buffer
area typically extending between two to five kilometers from the restricted boundary.
Drone operations within the inner yellow zone are allowed only after obtaining prior
permission from DGCA or local airspace authorities. Operators must submit flight plans in
advance, specifying flight altitude, duration, and intended purpose. Additional conditions
such as maximum permissible height and restricted flight paths are often imposed to avoid
interference with manned aircraft operations. In some cases, a Notice to Airmen (NOTAM)
must be filed before conducting any flight mission in this zone.
Beyond the inner yellow region lies the Outer Yellow Zone, extending roughly between
five and twelve kilometers from the red zone boundary. Drone operations in this area are
moderately regulated and may be permitted automatically through the Digital Sky
Platform, the official DGCA online portal for unmanned aircraft management. Although
automated permissions simplify the process, operators must still comply with operational
limitations, such as maintaining a safe distance from populated areas and flying only within

C. Pilot Licensing and Type Certification for Drones in India
1) Remote Pilot Certificate (RPC) / License
Under the Drone Rules, 2021 by the Directorate General of Civil Aviation (DGCA), every
individual intending to operate a drone—except nano-category drones under specific
exemptions—must possess a Remote Pilot Certificate (RPC). The RPC is the legal
authorization that certifies an individual’s competency to fly drones safely and responsibly
within Indian airspace. It replaces the earlier Remote Pilot License (RPL) terminology and
is managed through the Digital Sky Platform (DSP), which serves as the official online
interface for drone registration and operations in India.

medical compliance.
Category 3 – Advanced RPC:
Reserved for special and large drone operations, including those in defense, logistics, or
industrial automation, where advanced autonomous or BVLOS operations are conducted.

due to safety or regulatory violations.

D. Type Certification (TC) for Drones
Definition and Purpose
A Type Certificate (TC) is a regulatory authorization issued by DGCA to drone
manufacturers, confirming that the design and production of a specific UAV model meet
the prescribed airworthiness, safety, and performance standards. In India, as per Rule 7 of
the Drone Rules, 2021, no unmanned aircraft system can be operated unless it has obtained
a valid Type Certificate—unless specifically exempted.
Necessity and Exemptions
Type Certification ensures that drones meet technical, operational, and safety compliance
standards. It is mandatory for all drone models intended for commercial use. However,
exemptions exist for:
● Nano drones (below 250 g)
● Research and development prototypes
● Exhibition and demonstration models under controlled environments
Type Certification Process
The process involves multiple stages:
1. Application Submission:
The manufacturer submits a detailed application via the Digital Sky Platform,
including design documentation, compliance reports, and performance data.
2. Prototype Testing and Evaluation:
The drone undergoes testing by DGCA-recognized laboratories or Quality
Council of India (QCI) accredited agencies. Evaluations include mechanical
integrity, endurance, safety mechanisms, and electromagnetic compatibility
(EMC/EMI).
3. Regulatory Review:
DGCA assesses all submitted documentation, verifies compliance with
performance and safety standards, and ensures conformity with the Indian UAS
Certification Framework.
4. Issuance of Type Certificate:
Once compliance is confirmed, DGCA issues a Type Certificate. Any design
modification post-certification requires revalidation or amendment approval.

Delivery of Emergency Supplies: In disaster-stricken regions, drones can transport
essential supplies such as food, water, medicines, and first-aid kits to areas inaccessible by
road. Hybrid and multi-rotor drones with payload capabilities enable timely relief delivery,
especially in isolated communities or regions cut off by natural calamities. Temperature-
controlled payload compartments are used for transporting medical supplies, vaccines, or
blood, ensuring their integrity during transit.
Monitoring and Coordination: Drones provide live aerial footage to emergency response
teams and command centers, aiding in decision-making and coordination of rescue efforts.
By streaming real-time data, drones enable authorities to track ongoing operations, identify
potential hazards, and plan evacuation routes efficiently. Integration with Geographic
Information Systems (GIS) allows for mapping disaster zones and monitoring changes in
terrain or water levels during flood events.
Post-Disaster Analysis: Beyond immediate rescue operations, drones are used for post-
disaster assessment, infrastructure inspection, and rebuilding planning. They generate 3D
maps, topographic models, and detailed imagery for evaluating structural damage, planning
reconstruction, and assessing environmental impact.
